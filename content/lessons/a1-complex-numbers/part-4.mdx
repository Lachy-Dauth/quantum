## Why Quantum Mechanics Needs Complex Numbers

Throughout this lesson, we've built up the machinery of complex numbers — the Argand plane, arithmetic as geometry, Euler's formula. All elegant mathematics, but why should someone interested in quantum computing *care*?

Here's the short answer: quantum states are described by complex numbers, and the phase (the angle) of those complex numbers is what makes quantum computing work.

Let's see how this plays out, using only the tools we've developed in this lesson. No quantum formalism is required — just the idea that quantum mechanics uses complex amplitudes.

### States as complex vectors

In quantum computing, the simplest system is a **qubit**: a quantum bit that can be in a superposition of two states, which we label $\ket{0}$ and $\ket{1}$. (Don't worry about the bracket notation for now — we'll develop it formally in lesson A2. For now, just think of $\ket{0}$ and $\ket{1}$ as two basis directions, like "right" and "up" in the plane.)

A general qubit state is written as:

$$
\ket{\psi} = \alpha\ket{0} + \beta\ket{1}
$$

where $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are **complex numbers** called **amplitudes**, subject to the constraint:

$$
|\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1
$$

When you measure the qubit, the probability of getting outcome $0$ is $|\alpha|^2$ and the probability of getting outcome $1$ is $|\beta|^2$. This is the **Born rule**, and it's the bridge between the complex world of quantum states and the real world of experimental results.

The key observation: **probabilities depend on the modulus squared** $|\alpha|^2$, not on $\alpha$ itself. This means there's information in $\alpha$ that doesn't show up directly in measurement probabilities — namely, the *phase* of $\alpha$.

---

## Global Phase: The Direction That Doesn't Matter

Imagine you have a qubit in state $\ket{\psi} = \alpha\ket{0} + \beta\ket{1}$. Now multiply the entire state by some complex number of modulus 1 — say, $e^{i\phi}$:

$$
e^{i\phi}\ket{\psi} = e^{i\phi}\alpha\ket{0} + e^{i\phi}\beta\ket{1}
$$

What are the measurement probabilities now?

- Probability of $\ket{0}$: $|e^{i\phi}\alpha|^2 = |e^{i\phi}|^2 |\alpha|^2 = 1 \cdot |\alpha|^2 = |\alpha|^2$
- Probability of $\ket{1}$: $|e^{i\phi}\beta|^2 = |\beta|^2$

They're identical. Multiplying the entire state by $e^{i\phi}$ doesn't change any observable quantity. This overall factor $e^{i\phi}$ is called a **global phase**, and it's physically meaningless.

<Definition term="Global and Relative Phase">
  Given a quantum state $\ket{\psi} = \alpha\ket{0} + \beta\ket{1}$:

  **Global phase:** An overall factor $e^{i\phi}$ multiplying the entire state. The states $\ket{\psi}$ and $e^{i\phi}\ket{\psi}$ are physically indistinguishable — they give exactly the same measurement probabilities in every possible experiment.

  **Relative phase:** The phase difference between the amplitudes, $\arg(\beta) - \arg(\alpha)$. This *is* physically meaningful and can be detected through interference experiments.
</Definition>

### Why global phase vanishes

The reason global phase is unobservable is deeply connected to the modulus. When we compute $|e^{i\phi} \alpha|^2$, the Euler factor drops out because $|e^{i\phi}| = 1$. Every physical prediction in quantum mechanics goes through the Born rule ($|\text{amplitude}|^2$), and the modulus-squared operation erases global phase.

This is why, in Part 1, we emphasised the equation $z z^* = |z|^2$. In quantum mechanics, this identity is the mechanism by which global phase disappears: $(\alpha e^{i\phi})(\alpha e^{i\phi})^* = \alpha e^{i\phi} \cdot \alpha^* e^{-i\phi} = |\alpha|^2$.

## Relative Phase: The Direction That Does Matter

Now consider something different. Instead of multiplying the entire state by $e^{i\phi}$, suppose we change only the phase of the $\ket{1}$ amplitude:

$$
\ket{\psi_\theta} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\ket{0} + \frac{e^{i\theta}}{\sqrt{2}}\ket{1}
$$

For any value of $\theta$, the measurement probabilities in the $\{\ket{0}, \ket{1}\}$ basis are:

$$
P(0) = \left|\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right|^2 = \frac{1}{2}, \qquad P(1) = \left|\frac{e^{i\theta}}{\sqrt{2}}\right|^2 = \frac{1}{2}
$$

So in the standard basis, all values of $\theta$ look the same. This might tempt you to conclude that $\theta$ doesn't matter at all.

**But it does.** It just doesn't show up in *this* particular measurement.

### Interference reveals the phase

There's another way to measure a qubit — in the **Hadamard basis**. Define two new basis states:

$$
\ket{+} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{0} + \ket{1}), \qquad \ket{-} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{0} - \ket{1})
$$

(Think of these as "diagonal" directions, at 45° to the original axes.)

To find the probability of measuring $\ket{+}$, we need the inner product $\braket{+}{\psi_\theta}$ (which we'll define rigorously in lesson A2, but intuitively it measures the "overlap" between two states):

$$
\braket{+}{\psi_\theta} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} + \frac{e^{i\theta}}{\sqrt{2}}\right) = \frac{1 + e^{i\theta}}{2}
$$

The probability is:

$$
P(+) = \left|\frac{1 + e^{i\theta}}{2}\right|^2
$$

Let's compute this using the tools from this lesson. Write $1 + e^{i\theta} = 1 + \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$. Then:

$$
|1 + e^{i\theta}|^2 = (1 + \cos\theta)^2 + \sin^2\theta = 1 + 2\cos\theta + \cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 2 + 2\cos\theta
$$

Using the double-angle identity $\cos\theta = 2\cos^2(\theta/2) - 1$:

$$
|1 + e^{i\theta}|^2 = 2 + 2(2\cos^2(\theta/2) - 1) = 4\cos^2(\theta/2)
$$

So:

$$
P(+) = \frac{4\cos^2(\theta/2)}{4} = \cos^2\frac{\theta}{2}
$$

**This depends on $\theta$!** When $\theta = 0$ (the amplitudes are in phase), $P(+) = 1$ — certainty. When $\theta = \pi$ (the amplitudes are out of phase), $P(+) = 0$ — impossibility. The relative phase controls whether the amplitudes **constructively interfere** (adding up) or **destructively interfere** (cancelling out).

This is the single most important idea in this entire lesson, so let's say it again:

> **Global phase** (multiplying the whole state by $e^{i\phi}$) is unobservable. **Relative phase** (different amplitudes having different phases) is observable through interference. The entire power of quantum computing comes from manipulating relative phases.

<Callout type="confusion" title="&quot;If phase doesn't matter, why track it?&quot;">
  This is one of the most common confusions in quantum computing. Students hear "global phase is unobservable" and generalise to "all phase is unobservable." But global and relative phase are fundamentally different things.

  An analogy: suppose you're at a concert and two speakers are playing the same note. If you shift *both* speakers forward by the same amount, the sound doesn't change (global phase). But if you shift only *one* speaker, the sound changes dramatically — at some positions the waves reinforce each other (loud), at others they cancel (silence). That's interference, and it's the relative shift between the speakers that matters, not the absolute position of either one.

  The Hadamard gate (lesson C3) is the quantum equivalent of "measuring the interference pattern." It converts relative phase into measurement probability differences. Without it, all quantum computation would reduce to classical coin-flipping.
</Callout>

### Easy

<Problem id="a1-1" type="free-response">
  **Problem A1.1.** Convert $z = -1 + i\sqrt{3}$ to polar form. Give the modulus and principal argument.
</Problem>

<Proof>
  **Solution.**

  The modulus is $|z| = \sqrt{(-1)^2 + (\sqrt{3})^2} = \sqrt{1 + 3} = 2$.

  The point $(-1, \sqrt{3})$ is in the second quadrant. The reference angle is $\arctan(\sqrt{3}/1) = \pi/3$, so the argument is $\pi - \pi/3 = 2\pi/3$.

  Therefore $z = 2e^{i \cdot 2\pi/3}$.
</Proof>

<Problem id="a1-2" type="free-response">
  **Problem A1.2.** Compute $(2e^{i\pi/3})(3e^{i\pi/6})$ in both polar and rectangular form.
</Problem>

<Proof>
  **Solution.**

  **Polar:** Multiply moduli, add arguments: $|zw| = 2 \times 3 = 6$, $\arg(zw) = \pi/3 + \pi/6 = \pi/2$.

  So $zw = 6e^{i\pi/2} = 6i$.

  **Rectangular verification:** $2e^{i\pi/3} = 2(\cos(\pi/3) + i\sin(\pi/3)) = 2(1/2 + i\sqrt{3}/2) = 1 + i\sqrt{3}$.

  $3e^{i\pi/6} = 3(\cos(\pi/6) + i\sin(\pi/6)) = 3(\sqrt{3}/2 + i/2) = 3\sqrt{3}/2 + 3i/2$.

  Product: $(1 + i\sqrt{3})(3\sqrt{3}/2 + 3i/2)$.

  $= 3\sqrt{3}/2 + 3i/2 + 3i \cdot 3/2 + i\sqrt{3} \cdot 3i/2$

  $= 3\sqrt{3}/2 + 3i/2 + 3i\sqrt{3} \cdot \sqrt{3}/2 \cdot i$... Let's be more careful.

  Actually, the quickest verification: $6e^{i\pi/2} = 6(\cos(\pi/2) + i\sin(\pi/2)) = 6(0 + i) = 6i$. Since both forms give $6i$, confirmed.
</Proof>

<Problem id="a1-3" type="free-response">
  **Problem A1.3.** Prove that $|z^*| = |z|$ for all $z \in \C$.
</Problem>

<Proof>
  **Solution.**

  Let $z = a + bi$. Then $z^* = a - bi$.

  $|z^*| = \sqrt{a^2 + (-b)^2} = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2} = |z|$.

  The modulus depends on $b^2$, not on $b$, so flipping the sign of $b$ doesn't change it.
</Proof>

### Medium

<Problem id="a1-4" type="free-response">
  **Problem A1.4.** Find all $z$ such that $z^6 = -1$. Express your answers in exponential form and describe their geometric arrangement.
</Problem>

<Proof>
  **Solution.**

  Write $-1 = e^{i\pi}$. Then $z^6 = e^{i\pi}$ requires $z = e^{i(\pi + 2\pi k)/6}$ for $k = 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5$.

  The six solutions are:

  | $k$ | $\theta = \frac{\pi + 2\pi k}{6}$ | Simplified |
  |---|---|---|
  | 0 | $\pi/6$ | $e^{i\pi/6}$ |
  | 1 | $\pi/2$ | $e^{i\pi/2} = i$ |
  | 2 | $5\pi/6$ | $e^{i5\pi/6}$ |
  | 3 | $7\pi/6$ | $e^{i7\pi/6}$ |
  | 4 | $3\pi/2$ | $e^{i3\pi/2} = -i$ |
  | 5 | $11\pi/6$ | $e^{i11\pi/6}$ |

  These form a regular hexagon inscribed in the unit circle, rotated by $\pi/6$ (30°) from the standard position (where the vertices would be the 6th roots of unity).
</Proof>

<Problem id="a1-5" type="free-response">
  **Problem A1.5.** Prove the parallelogram law: $|z + w|^2 + |z - w|^2 = 2(|z|^2 + |w|^2)$.
</Problem>

<Proof>
  **Solution.**

  Expand each term using $|\alpha|^2 = \alpha \alpha^*$:

  $$
  |z + w|^2 = (z + w)(z^* + w^*) = |z|^2 + zw^* + wz^* + |w|^2
  $$

  $$
  |z - w|^2 = (z - w)(z^* - w^*) = |z|^2 - zw^* - wz^* + |w|^2
  $$

  Adding these:

  $$
  |z + w|^2 + |z - w|^2 = 2|z|^2 + 2|w|^2
  $$

  The cross terms $zw^* + wz^*$ cancel. This is the parallelogram law: the sum of the squares of the diagonals of a parallelogram equals the sum of the squares of all four sides.
</Proof>

<Problem id="a1-6" type="free-response">
  **Problem A1.6.** Show that for any $z \in \C$: $\operatorname{Re}(z) = \frac{z + z^*}{2}$ and $\operatorname{Im}(z) = \frac{z - z^*}{2i}$.
</Problem>

<Proof>
  **Solution.**

  Let $z = a + bi$.

  $z + z^* = (a + bi) + (a - bi) = 2a$, so $\operatorname{Re}(z) = \frac{z + z^*}{2} = a$. ✓

  $z - z^* = (a + bi) - (a - bi) = 2bi$, so $\operatorname{Im}(z) = \frac{z - z^*}{2i} = \frac{2bi}{2i} = b$. ✓

  Note: this derivation used no assumption about $|z|$; the identities hold for all $z \in \C$.
</Proof>

<Problem id="a1-7" type="free-response">
  **Problem A1.7.** The $T$ gate in quantum computing multiplies a qubit amplitude by $e^{i\pi/4}$. If the initial amplitude is $\alpha = \frac{1+i}{2}$, find the new amplitude $T\alpha$ and verify that $|T\alpha|^2 = |\alpha|^2$.
</Problem>

<Proof>
  **Solution.**

  First, $e^{i\pi/4} = \frac{1+i}{\sqrt{2}}$ (from the key values table).

  $$
  T\alpha = e^{i\pi/4} \cdot \frac{1+i}{2} = \frac{1+i}{\sqrt{2}} \cdot \frac{1+i}{2} = \frac{(1+i)^2}{2\sqrt{2}}
  $$

  Compute $(1+i)^2 = 1 + 2i + i^2 = 1 + 2i - 1 = 2i$.

  $$
  T\alpha = \frac{2i}{2\sqrt{2}} = \frac{i}{\sqrt{2}}
  $$

  **Verification:** $|T\alpha|^2 = \left|\frac{i}{\sqrt{2}}\right|^2 = \frac{|i|^2}{2} = \frac{1}{2}$.

  And $|\alpha|^2 = \left|\frac{1+i}{2}\right|^2 = \frac{|1+i|^2}{4} = \frac{2}{4} = \frac{1}{2}$. ✓

  Modulus preserved — as it must be, since $|e^{i\pi/4}| = 1$. Multiplication by a unit-modulus complex number preserves probabilities.
</Proof>

### Hard

<Problem id="a1-8" type="free-response">
  **Problem A1.8.** Prove that if $|z| = 1$ and $z \neq 1$, then $\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} z^k = \frac{1 - z^n}{1 - z}$. Use this to show that the $n$-th roots of unity (other than 1) are roots of $1 + z + z^2 + \cdots + z^{n-1} = 0$.
</Problem>

<Proof>
  **Solution.**

  **Part 1: Geometric series formula.**

  Let $S = 1 + z + z^2 + \cdots + z^{n-1}$. Multiply both sides by $z$:

  $$
  zS = z + z^2 + z^3 + \cdots + z^n
  $$

  Subtract: $S - zS = 1 - z^n$, so $S(1 - z) = 1 - z^n$.

  Since $z \neq 1$, we can divide: $S = \frac{1 - z^n}{1 - z}$.

  (Note: the condition $|z| = 1$ isn't needed for this formula — it holds for any $z \neq 1$.)

  **Part 2: Roots of the cyclotomic polynomial.**

  Let $\omega_j = e^{2\pi ij/n}$ with $j \neq 0$ (so $\omega_j \neq 1$). Then $\omega_j^n = e^{2\pi ij} = 1$, so the numerator $1 - \omega_j^n = 0$, and the denominator $1 - \omega_j \neq 0$ (since $\omega_j \neq 1$).

  Therefore $S = 0$, meaning $1 + \omega_j + \omega_j^2 + \cdots + \omega_j^{n-1} = 0$.

  So each non-trivial $n$-th root of unity is a root of the polynomial $p(z) = 1 + z + z^2 + \cdots + z^{n-1} = \frac{z^n - 1}{z - 1}$.
</Proof>

<Problem id="a1-9" type="free-response">
  **Problem A1.9.** Let $z = e^{i\theta}$. Express $\cos\theta$ and $\sin\theta$ in terms of $z$. Use this to prove $\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1$ purely algebraically.
</Problem>

<Proof>
  **Solution.**

  Since $z = e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$ and $z^{-1} = e^{-i\theta} = \cos\theta - i\sin\theta$:

  $$
  \cos\theta = \frac{z + z^{-1}}{2}, \qquad \sin\theta = \frac{z - z^{-1}}{2i}
  $$

  Now compute:

  $$
  \cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = \frac{(z + z^{-1})^2}{4} + \frac{(z - z^{-1})^2}{(2i)^2}
  $$

  Since $(2i)^2 = -4$:

  $$
  = \frac{(z + z^{-1})^2}{4} - \frac{(z - z^{-1})^2}{4} = \frac{(z + z^{-1})^2 - (z - z^{-1})^2}{4}
  $$

  Expand the numerator:

  $(z + z^{-1})^2 = z^2 + 2 + z^{-2}$

  $(z - z^{-1})^2 = z^2 - 2 + z^{-2}$

  Difference: $(z^2 + 2 + z^{-2}) - (z^2 - 2 + z^{-2}) = 4$.

  Therefore:

  $$
  \cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = \frac{4}{4} = 1
  $$

  The Pythagorean identity follows from pure algebra on complex exponentials — no geometry or trigonometric identities required.
</Proof>

<Problem id="a1-10" type="free-response">
  **Problem A1.10 (Preview).** Consider the state $\ket{\psi} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{0} + e^{i\theta}\ket{1})$.

  **(a)** Show that the measurement probabilities in the $\{\ket{0}, \ket{1}\}$ basis are independent of $\theta$.

  **(b)** The Hadamard basis states are $\ket{\pm} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{0} \pm \ket{1})$. Compute $P(+) = |\braket{+}{\psi}|^2$ and show that it depends on $\theta$.

  **(c)** For which values of $\theta$ is $P(+) = 1$? For which is $P(+) = 0$? What's happening physically in each case?
</Problem>

<Proof>
  **Solution.**

  **(a)** $P(0) = |1/\sqrt{2}|^2 = 1/2$ and $P(1) = |e^{i\theta}/\sqrt{2}|^2 = |e^{i\theta}|^2/2 = 1/2$, since $|e^{i\theta}| = 1$. Both probabilities are $1/2$ regardless of $\theta$. The computational basis measurement cannot distinguish different values of $\theta$.

  **(b)** $\braket{+}{\psi} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} + \frac{e^{i\theta}}{\sqrt{2}}\right) = \frac{1 + e^{i\theta}}{2}$.

  $$
  P(+) = \left|\frac{1 + e^{i\theta}}{2}\right|^2 = \frac{|1 + e^{i\theta}|^2}{4}
  $$

  Compute $|1 + e^{i\theta}|^2 = (1 + \cos\theta)^2 + \sin^2\theta = 2 + 2\cos\theta = 4\cos^2(\theta/2)$.

  Therefore: $P(+) = \cos^2(\theta/2)$.

  This depends on $\theta$, so the Hadamard basis *can* distinguish different relative phases.

  **(c)** $P(+) = 1$ when $\cos(\theta/2) = \pm 1$, i.e., $\theta = 0$ (or any multiple of $2\pi$). In this case $\ket{\psi} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{0} + \ket{1}) = \ket{+}$ — the amplitudes are perfectly in phase, and they constructively interfere to give certainty.

  $P(+) = 0$ when $\cos(\theta/2) = 0$, i.e., $\theta = \pi$. In this case $\ket{\psi} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{0} - \ket{1}) = \ket{-}$ — the amplitudes are perfectly out of phase, and they destructively interfere. You get $\ket{-}$ with certainty instead.

  This is quantum interference in its simplest form. The relative phase $\theta$ controls the interference pattern, turning certainty in one direction ($\ket{+}$) into certainty in the opposite direction ($\ket{-}$), with everything in between for intermediate values of $\theta$.
</Proof>
