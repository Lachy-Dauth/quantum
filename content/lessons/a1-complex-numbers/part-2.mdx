## Addition: The Parallelogram Rule

Let's start with the simplest operation. To add two complex numbers, you add their real parts and their imaginary parts separately:

$$
(a + bi) + (c + di) = (a + c) + (b + d)i
$$

Algebraically, this is straightforward. But the geometry is more revealing.

Since each complex number is a vector (an arrow from the origin to a point), addition of complex numbers is **vector addition**. If you place the vectors $z$ and $w$ as two sides of a parallelogram starting from the origin, then $z + w$ is the diagonal of that parallelogram.

You might remember this from physics as the parallelogram rule for adding forces or velocities. The same picture works here: to add $z$ and $w$, slide $w$ so its tail is at the tip of $z$ (or equivalently, slide $z$ so its tail is at the tip of $w$). The sum $z + w$ is where you end up.

Subtraction is the reverse: $z - w$ is the vector from $w$ to $z$. If you draw $z$ and $w$ both starting from the origin, then $z - w$ connects their tips.

This geometric picture makes some things immediately obvious:

- **$z + 0 = z$**: adding zero doesn't move you anywhere.
- **$z + (-z) = 0$**: the vector $-z$ points in the opposite direction from $z$, so they cancel.
- **Addition is commutative**: $z + w = w + z$, because the parallelogram is the same regardless of which vector you draw first.

## Multiplication: Rotate and Scale

Addition was simple — add the coordinates. Multiplication is where complex numbers become genuinely surprising.

Let's start with the algebra. To multiply $(a + bi)(c + di)$, you expand using the distributive law, just as you would with any binomials, and then use the rule $i^2 = -1$:

$$
(a + bi)(c + di) = ac + adi + bci + bdi^2 = (ac - bd) + (ad + bc)i
$$

That formula looks complicated. But the geometric interpretation is beautiful.

### The polar form makes it clear

Suppose we write both numbers in polar form. Let $z = r_1(\cos\theta_1 + i\sin\theta_1)$ and $w = r_2(\cos\theta_2 + i\sin\theta_2)$. What is $z \cdot w$?

$$
zw = r_1 r_2 \left[(\cos\theta_1\cos\theta_2 - \sin\theta_1\sin\theta_2) + i(\cos\theta_1\sin\theta_2 + \sin\theta_1\cos\theta_2)\right]
$$

If you recognise the angle-addition formulas from trigonometry, the expression in brackets simplifies dramatically:

$$
zw = r_1 r_2 \left[\cos(\theta_1 + \theta_2) + i\sin(\theta_1 + \theta_2)\right]
$$

In other words:

> **To multiply two complex numbers, multiply their moduli and add their arguments.**

$$
|zw| = |z| \cdot |w|, \qquad \arg(zw) = \arg(z) + \arg(w)
$$

This is the fundamental insight. Multiplication by a complex number is a **rotation** (by the argument) combined with a **scaling** (by the modulus). When you multiply by a number on the unit circle ($|w| = 1$), there's no scaling — it's a pure rotation. When you multiply by a positive real number, there's no rotation — it's a pure scaling.

This explains why $i$ is a 90-degree rotation: $i$ has modulus 1 and argument $\pi/2$, so multiplying by $i$ rotates by $\pi/2$ and doesn't change the length.

<Callout type="confusion" title="Confusing argument addition with modulus addition">

A common mistake: students write $|zw| = |z| + |w|$ (adding the moduli) or $\arg(zw) = \arg(z) \cdot \arg(w)$ (multiplying the arguments). The correct rule is the opposite: **moduli multiply, arguments add**.

A helpful mnemonic: multiplication of complex numbers is like multiplication of powers. Just as $x^a \cdot x^b = x^{a+b}$ (the exponents add), the arguments (which are like exponents in the exponential form $re^{i\theta}$) add under multiplication. And just as $(xy)^a = x^a y^a$ (the bases multiply), the moduli multiply.

</Callout>

### Why this works: the algebraic derivation

Let's prove the multiplication rule from scratch, without relying on the trig addition formulas. This approach will reinforce both the algebra and the geometry.

Start with the rectangular formula: $(a + bi)(c + di) = (ac - bd) + (ad + bc)i$.

Compute the modulus of the product:

$$
|zw|^2 = (ac - bd)^2 + (ad + bc)^2
$$

Expand:

$$
= a^2c^2 - 2abcd + b^2d^2 + a^2d^2 + 2abcd + b^2c^2
$$

The $-2abcd$ and $+2abcd$ cancel:

$$
= a^2c^2 + b^2d^2 + a^2d^2 + b^2c^2 = (a^2 + b^2)(c^2 + d^2) = |z|^2 |w|^2
$$

Taking square roots: $|zw| = |z||w|$. Moduli multiply. This is a clean algebraic fact that doesn't depend on polar form at all.

For the argument, note that if $z$ has argument $\theta_1$ and $w$ has argument $\theta_2$, then the trig identity $\cos(\theta_1 + \theta_2) + i\sin(\theta_1 + \theta_2)$ matches the formula for the product's rectangular coordinates when both numbers are on the unit circle. The general case follows by factoring out the moduli.

<WorkedExample title="Geometric Multiplication">

**Compute $(1 + i)(\sqrt{3} + i)$ and interpret geometrically.**

**Step 1: Convert to polar form.**

For $1 + i$: modulus $= \sqrt{1^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{2}$, argument $= \pi/4$ (45 degrees, first quadrant).

For $\sqrt{3} + i$: modulus $= \sqrt{3 + 1} = 2$, argument $= \pi/6$ (since $\tan\theta = 1/\sqrt{3}$, so $\theta = 30°$).

**Step 2: Multiply in polar form.**

$$
|zw| = \sqrt{2} \cdot 2 = 2\sqrt{2}, \qquad \arg(zw) = \frac{\pi}{4} + \frac{\pi}{6} = \frac{3\pi + 2\pi}{12} = \frac{5\pi}{12}
$$

So the product has modulus $2\sqrt{2}$ and argument $5\pi/12$ (75 degrees).

**Step 3: Verify in rectangular form.**

$$
(1 + i)(\sqrt{3} + i) = \sqrt{3} + i + i\sqrt{3} + i^2 = (\sqrt{3} - 1) + (1 + \sqrt{3})i
$$

Check the modulus: $(\sqrt{3} - 1)^2 + (1 + \sqrt{3})^2 = (3 - 2\sqrt{3} + 1) + (1 + 2\sqrt{3} + 3) = 8$.

So $|zw| = \sqrt{8} = 2\sqrt{2}$. Confirmed.

**The geometric picture:** We took a vector of length $\sqrt{2}$ at 45° and a vector of length $2$ at 30°, and the product is a vector of length $2\sqrt{2}$ at 75°. The multiplication rotated and scaled exactly as the rule predicts.

</WorkedExample>

<Callout type="foreshadow" title="Looking ahead: quantum gates">

In quantum computing, single-qubit gates are complex numbers of modulus 1 (or 2×2 matrices of such numbers). Applying a gate to a qubit amplitude is exactly this "rotate without scaling" operation. The phase gates $S$ and $T$ (lesson C3) multiply amplitudes by $e^{i\pi/2} = i$ and $e^{i\pi/4}$ respectively — pure rotations by 90° and 45°.

</Callout>

## Division: Rotating the Other Way

If multiplication rotates and scales, then division should _undo_ a rotation and scaling. And it does: to divide $z$ by $w$, you divide the moduli and subtract the arguments:

$$
\left|\frac{z}{w}\right| = \frac{|z|}{|w|}, \qquad \arg\left(\frac{z}{w}\right) = \arg(z) - \arg(w)
$$

But what about the rectangular formula? If you want to compute $\frac{a + bi}{c + di}$, you can't just divide the real parts and imaginary parts separately (that doesn't work). Instead, you use the **conjugate trick**: multiply both the numerator and denominator by the conjugate of the denominator.

$$
\frac{a + bi}{c + di} = \frac{(a + bi)(c - di)}{(c + di)(c - di)} = \frac{(a + bi)(c - di)}{c^2 + d^2}
$$

The denominator becomes $c^2 + d^2 = |w|^2$, which is a real number — no more $i$ in the denominator. The numerator you expand normally, and then separate into real and imaginary parts.

Why does this work? Because $w \cdot w^* = |w|^2$ is always a positive real number. Multiplying numerator and denominator by $w^*$ clears the complex denominator.

<WorkedExample title="Conjugate Simplification">

**Simplify $\displaystyle\frac{3 + 4i}{1 - 2i}$ and find its modulus.**

**Step 1: Multiply by the conjugate of the denominator.**

$$
\frac{3 + 4i}{1 - 2i} \cdot \frac{1 + 2i}{1 + 2i} = \frac{(3 + 4i)(1 + 2i)}{(1 - 2i)(1 + 2i)}
$$

**Step 2: Compute the denominator.**

$$
(1 - 2i)(1 + 2i) = 1^2 + 2^2 = 5
$$

(using $w \cdot w^* = |w|^2$).

**Step 3: Compute the numerator.**

$$
(3 + 4i)(1 + 2i) = 3 + 6i + 4i + 8i^2 = 3 + 10i - 8 = -5 + 10i
$$

**Step 4: Combine.**

$$
\frac{-5 + 10i}{5} = -1 + 2i
$$

**Step 5: Find the modulus.**

$$
|-1 + 2i| = \sqrt{1 + 4} = \sqrt{5}
$$

**Cross-check via the modulus quotient rule:**

$$
\frac{|3 + 4i|}{|1 - 2i|} = \frac{\sqrt{9 + 16}}{\sqrt{1 + 4}} = \frac{5}{\sqrt{5}} = \sqrt{5} \quad \checkmark
$$

The modulus cross-check is a powerful sanity check. If the modulus of your answer doesn't match $|z|/|w|$, you've made an arithmetic error somewhere.

</WorkedExample>

## The Conjugate and Its Properties

The complex conjugate keeps showing up — in division, in the modulus formula $z z^* = |z|^2$, and (as we'll see later) all throughout quantum mechanics. Let's catalogue its key properties.

<Theorem name="Conjugate Identities">

For any complex numbers $z$ and $w$:

1. $(z + w)^* = z^* + w^*$ — the conjugate of a sum is the sum of the conjugates
2. $(zw)^* = z^* w^*$ — the conjugate of a product is the product of the conjugates
3. $z z^* = |z|^2$ — a number times its conjugate is the square of its modulus
4. $(z^*)^* = z$ — conjugating twice gives you back the original

</Theorem>

<Proof>

Let $z = a + bi$ and $w = c + di$.

**(1)** $(z + w)^* = ((a+c) + (b+d)i)^* = (a+c) - (b+d)i = (a - bi) + (c - di) = z^* + w^*$.

**(2)** $zw = (ac - bd) + (ad + bc)i$, so $(zw)^* = (ac - bd) - (ad + bc)i$. Meanwhile, $z^* w^* = (a - bi)(c - di) = (ac - bd) + (-ad - bc)i = (ac - bd) - (ad + bc)i$. They match.

**(3)** $z z^* = (a + bi)(a - bi) = a^2 - (bi)^2 = a^2 - b^2 i^2 = a^2 + b^2 = |z|^2$.

**(4)** $(z^*)^* = (a - bi)^* = a + bi = z$.

</Proof>

<Callout type="confusion" title="Confusing z* with -z">

Students sometimes mix up the conjugate $z^*$ with the negation $-z$. They are different operations:

- **Conjugate** $z^* = a - bi$: flips the imaginary part. Geometrically, this is a **reflection across the real axis** (flip up/down).
- **Negation** $-z = -a - bi$: flips both parts. Geometrically, this is a **rotation by 180°** (or equivalently, reflection through the origin).

Quick check: $(3 + 4i)^* = 3 - 4i$, but $-(3 + 4i) = -3 - 4i$. The conjugate keeps the real part; negation changes it.

Geometrically: the conjugate of a point in the upper half-plane is the corresponding point in the lower half-plane (same horizontal position, reflected vertically). Negation sends it to the diagonally opposite point.

</Callout>

### The modulus is multiplicative

The conjugate identities let us prove an important property of the modulus cleanly.

**Proposition (Modulus is Multiplicative).** For any complex numbers $z$ and $w$, $|zw| = |z| \cdot |w|$.

<Proof>

We compute $|zw|^2$ using the identity $\alpha \alpha^* = |\alpha|^2$ with $\alpha = zw$:

$$
|zw|^2 = (zw)(zw)^* = (zw)(z^* w^*) = (z z^*)(w w^*) = |z|^2 |w|^2
$$

where we used the fact that $(zw)^* = z^* w^*$ (conjugate identity 2) and the commutativity and associativity of multiplication to rearrange.

Since $|zw|^2 = |z|^2 |w|^2$ and all these quantities are non-negative, taking square roots gives $|zw| = |z| \cdot |w|$.

</Proof>

Notice how much cleaner this proof is when you use the conjugate identities compared to the brute-force expansion we did earlier. The conjugate is a bookkeeping tool that keeps track of moduli without forcing you to expand everything into real and imaginary parts.

## The Triangle Inequality

Here's a fact that you already know geometrically, even if you haven't seen it stated for complex numbers.

<Theorem name="Triangle Inequality">

For any complex numbers $z$ and $w$:

$$
|z + w| \leq |z| + |w|
$$

Equality holds if and only if one of $z$, $w$ is a non-negative real multiple of the other (i.e., they point in the same direction from the origin).

</Theorem>

Geometrically, this says that the length of one side of a triangle is at most the sum of the lengths of the other two sides. In the complex plane, the three vertices of the triangle are $0$, $z$, and $z + w$. The side from $0$ to $z$ has length $|z|$, the side from $z$ to $z + w$ has length $|w|$, and the side from $0$ to $z + w$ has length $|z + w|$.

The direct path (from $0$ straight to $z + w$) can never be longer than the two-step path (from $0$ to $z$, then from $z$ to $z + w$). You can't create a shortcut by taking a detour.

/>

<Proof>

We prove this algebraically. Start by squaring both sides (both sides are non-negative, so this is valid):

$$
|z + w|^2 \leq (|z| + |w|)^2
$$

**Left side:** Expand using $|\alpha|^2 = \alpha \alpha^*$:

$$
|z + w|^2 = (z + w)(z + w)^* = (z + w)(z^* + w^*) = zz^* + zw^* + wz^* + ww^*
$$

$$
= |z|^2 + |w|^2 + zw^* + (zw^*)^*
$$

Now, for any complex number $\alpha$, we have $\alpha + \alpha^* = 2\operatorname{Re}(\alpha)$. So:

$$
|z + w|^2 = |z|^2 + |w|^2 + 2\operatorname{Re}(zw^*)
$$

**Right side:**

$$
(|z| + |w|)^2 = |z|^2 + 2|z||w| + |w|^2
$$

**Comparing:** We need to show that $\operatorname{Re}(zw^*) \leq |zw^*|$. But this is always true — the real part of any complex number is at most its modulus (since $\operatorname{Re}(\alpha) \leq |\alpha|$). And $|zw^*| = |z||w^*| = |z||w|$ (since $|w^*| = |w|$).

Therefore:

$$
|z + w|^2 = |z|^2 + |w|^2 + 2\operatorname{Re}(zw^*) \leq |z|^2 + |w|^2 + 2|z||w| = (|z| + |w|)^2
$$

Taking square roots gives $|z + w| \leq |z| + |w|$.

**Equality condition:** Equality holds when $\operatorname{Re}(zw^*) = |zw^*|$, which means $zw^*$ is a non-negative real number, which means $z$ and $w$ point in the same direction (or one of them is zero).

</Proof>

<Callout type="foreshadow" title="Looking ahead: inner products and distances">

The triangle inequality is one of the defining properties of a _norm_ — a way of measuring length.
In lesson A2, we'll define inner products and norms on complex vector spaces, and the triangle
inequality will reappear in a more general form. Every distance-like concept in quantum mechanics
ultimately inherits from this inequality.

</Callout>

## Extracting Real and Imaginary Parts

Here's a pair of identities that connect rectangular form to the conjugate. They're simple but surprisingly useful.

For any complex number $z = a + bi$:

$$
\operatorname{Re}(z) = \frac{z + z^*}{2}, \qquad \operatorname{Im}(z) = \frac{z - z^*}{2i}
$$

The proof is immediate: $z + z^* = (a + bi) + (a - bi) = 2a$, so $\operatorname{Re}(z) = (z + z^*)/2$. Similarly, $z - z^* = (a + bi) - (a - bi) = 2bi$, so $\operatorname{Im}(z) = (z - z^*)/(2i)$.

These identities are useful because they let you express $\operatorname{Re}$ and $\operatorname{Im}$ purely in terms of $z$ and $z^*$, without needing to decompose $z$ into $a$ and $b$ first. We'll use them in Part 3 when we relate $\cos\theta$ and $\sin\theta$ to $e^{i\theta}$.

## Summary of Arithmetic Operations

Let's collect everything in one place.

| Operation          | Rectangular                          | Polar                                  |
| ------------------ | ------------------------------------ | -------------------------------------- |
| **Addition**       | $(a+bi) + (c+di) = (a+c) + (b+d)i$   | (No clean polar formula)               |
| **Subtraction**    | $(a+bi) - (c+di) = (a-c) + (b-d)i$   | (No clean polar formula)               |
| **Multiplication** | $(a+bi)(c+di) = (ac-bd) + (ad+bc)i$  | Multiply moduli, add arguments         |
| **Division**       | Multiply by conjugate of denominator | Divide moduli, subtract arguments      |
| **Conjugation**    | $(a+bi)^* = a - bi$                  | $r e^{i\theta} \mapsto r e^{-i\theta}$ |

Notice the pattern: **addition and subtraction are easier in rectangular form** (just add the components), while **multiplication and division are easier in polar form** (just combine the moduli and arguments). Knowing when to use which form is a skill that comes with practice.
